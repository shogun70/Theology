My theory, then, is that canonical Mark dismembered Secret Mark's story of the young man's resurrection and initiation

But that raises a question I did not realize earlier.
When you remove those elements, what is actually left for a conclusion to Secret Mark?
...
Did Secret Mark conclude with any story about finding the empty tomb?
The obvious answer is that of course it did. There are still the women.
But now I notice a curious coincidence that I missed before. 
There are three women identified in Secret Mark 2r14–16 as “the sister of the youth whom Jesus loved and his mother and Salome”.
There are also three women mentioned in Mark 15:40 as “Mary Magdalene, and Mary the mother of James the younger and of Joses, and Salome”
...
Is that too much coincidence?
...
I think that, once again, canonical Mark has simply relocated the textual debris of a censored incident from Secret Mark.
...
The three women were not, any more than the young man in the tomb, part of the conclusion to Secret Mark.

Why did Mark go viral ...

despite not including resurrection appearances?

We find this surprising because we are looking backwards through the later gospels which included resurrection encounters. Mark's first audiences were perfectly fine not having those encounters in their Passion play.

The resurrection is an afterthought in Mark (even including the proposed postscript). Mark isn't trying to convince his audience that Jesus is the Son of God - he isn't writing that they may believe. Mark is reminding his audience how a true Messiah saves the world.

Conjecture: It had become traditional to have a strong separation between Jesus's suffering and the hope of resurrection. Perhaps originally the Markan community was recounting only the former before Passover and the latter only after. (We still do this.)

  • Mark, Ultimate edition
  • Changes some things to foreshadow the different ending.

Because we have Mark, Matthew, and Luke, we can see the editorial process is iterative - experimentation with different approaches; change things; fix things; forget to fix things.

But what if we didn't have Mark? How would we approach Matthew?

Timelines

The Day of Preparation (for the Sabbath)

Jesus is apparently crucified on the Day of Preparation but this detail is not divulged until it is mentioned as the reason to get Jesus off his cross.

After three days

On three occasions Jesus predicts he will rise after three days. Yet his death is the late in the day before the Sabbath and his resurrection no later than first thing on the day after the Sabbath.
Matthew and Luke change each prediction to on the third day.
John places the death at least a day earlier, either for theological reasons or perhaps to address this discrepancy between prediction and story.

Anointing the body

The women's reason for going to the tomb is to anoint the body, but you do this before burial.
Matthew addresses issue by not mentioning it. John "fixes" it by having Joseph and Nicodemus perform the anointing.

Moreover, this reason is doubly invalidated because Jesus was already anointed for burial in Bethany - a story which surely would not have been given such significance if Mark was thinking of the women at the tomb?
Or is this dramatic foreshadowing or insider knowledge?

Timestamps of Good Friday

There is a strong continuity of timestamps on the day of crucifixion:

And as soon as it was morning, the chief priests held a consultation with the elders and scribes and the whole council.
And they bound Jesus and led him away and delivered him over to Pilate.
...
And they crucified him and divided his garments among them, casting lots for them, to decide what each should take.
And it was the third hour when they crucified him.
...
And when the sixth hour had come, there was darkness over the whole land until the ninth hour.
And at the ninth hour Jesus cried with a loud voice, “Eloi, Eloi, lema sabachthani?”
...
And when the centurion, who stood facing him, saw that in this way he breathed his last, he said,
“Truly this man was the Son of God!”
...
And when evening had come, since it was the day of Preparation, that is, the day before the Sabbath, Joseph of Arimathea ...
took courage and went to Pilate and asked for the body of Jesus.

Once you see the pattern it is hard not to think that the timestamps were deliberately included to mark out a whole day, including the burial.
Doesn't that rule out the proposed postscript?
Maybe, but it could have been coincidence. Or the pattern could have been enhanced with some late additions into Mark 15.

The young man and his linen smock

Is the young man in a white robe at the empty tomb the same as the young man who loses his linen cloth when Jesus is arrested? It feels like he must be:

  • The young man following Jesus is wearing a linen cloth.
  • Jesus is wrapped in a linen cloth and buried in a tomb.
  • A young man is at the same tomb when the women find the body of Jesus is gone.
  • The young man fled naked - Jesus would have been crucified naked.

It feels like an example of Chekhov's gun somehow.

Remove everything that has no relevance to the story. If you say in the first act that there is a rifle hanging on the wall, in the second or third act it absolutely must go off. If it's not going to be fired, it shouldn't be hanging there.

But given that none of the later gospels retain this story - and given the numerous theories trying to explain it - it seems to be a link which Mark's contemporaries understood but quickly became confusing for later audiences.

If it is the same man then surely that confirms Mark was thinking about the empty tomb when he wrote the arrest scene?

Perhaps, but it is a strange tangent to the scene we find it in - it isn't impossible that it was inserted after the postscript was written. (Movies and plays will tweak earlier scenes when later scenes require it.)

No matter, let us consider what forms of manuscript evidence would be supportive of an earlier Mark without the suggested postscript, (ordered by approximate weight):

  • a fragment which ends at Mark 15:39.
  • a fragment of Mark 15 which does not contain the timestamps - the third hour, the sixth hour, the ninth hour.
  • an ending of Mark which doesn't mention the women at the cross.
  • a fragment of Mark 14 without v28 or v51.
  • an account of the Transfiguration or Jesus walking on the water which don't align with the gospels.
  • early christian writings which indicate that Mark underwent revisions. e.g. the Mar Saba letter, if found and accepted as plausibly Clementine, would support an early belief that Mark's gospel underwent revision.

Location of appearances

Mark foreshadows that Jesus resurrection encounters will be for the disciples (and the women?) in Galilee.

Matthew actually shows the disciples seeing Jesus in Galilee. But the resurrection action is actually weighted towards Jerusalem: the women meet Jesus; saints are resurrected; tomb guards witness angels and Jesus meeting the women.

Luke modifies Jesus's words so that the encounters are foreshadowed for Jerusalem, and all the encounters are in or near Jerusalem.

John similarly has resurrection encounters in Jerusalem with the exception of the likely postscript of John 21.

Nature of appearances

Mark

  • Foreshadowed (not explicit)
  • Visible in a location

Matthew

  • Seen, heard, worshipped by the disciples (but some doubted)
  • Seen, heard, touched, worshipped by the women (no doubts about it)

Luke

  • A stranger on the road (reminiscent of transmigration)
  • An unelaborated vision to Peter
  • A visitation to the disciples. Talks. Flesh and bones. Eats. Has crucifixion wounds. Revivification plus?
  • Taken up to heaven (ambiguous).

Acts

  • Eats, talks.
  • Taken up into the heavens. Obscured by a cloud.

John

  • Seen, touched, talked to, by Mary Magdalene.
  • A visitation to the disciples in a locked room. Talks. Has crucifixion wounds.
    Heavenly-body, earthly-body fusion? Fusion body?
  • A similar visitation to the disciples this time with the doubting Thomas.
    He is told to stop doubting and he emphatically does.
  • A stranger on the shores of the Sea of Galilee whom the disciples infer to be Jesus.

Resurrection outside the NT

Josephus - War of the Jews - Book 2

Historians seem to be in disagreement whether Josephus is reliable regarding religious beliefs, but the inescapable conclusion is that there is debate and a range of theories, apparently with a high correspondence to Paul's audience in 1 Corinthians 15.

Essenes

That bodies are corruptible ... but that the souls are immortal ... and that they come out of the most subtil air
but that when they are set free from the bonds of the flesh ... rejoice and mount upward.

Greeks

And indeed the Greeks seem to me to have followed the same notion,
when they allot the islands of the blessed to their brave men ...
and to the souls of the wicked, the region of the ungodly in Hades ...
which is built on this first supposition, that souls are immortal;
and thence are those exhortations to virtue, and dehortations from wickedness collected;
whereby good men are bettered in the conduct of their life by the hope they have of reward after their death,
and whereby the vehement inclinations of bad men to vice are restrained,
by the fear and expectation ... that ... they should suffer immortal punishment after their death.

Pharisees

They say that all souls are incorruptible,
but that the souls of good men only are removed into other bodies,
but that the souls of bad men are subject to eternal punishment.

Saducees

To act what is good, or what is evil, is at men’s own choice ...
They also take away the belief of the immortal duration of the soul, and the punishments and rewards in Hades.
