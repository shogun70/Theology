Why did Mark go viral ...

despite not including resurrection appearances?

We find this surprising because we are looking backwards through the later gospels which included resurrection encounters. Mark's first audiences were perfectly fine not having those encounters in their Passion play.

The resurrection is an afterthought in Mark (even including the proposed postscript). Mark isn't trying to convince his audience that Jesus is the Son of God - he isn't writing that they may believe. Mark is reminding his audience how a true Messiah saves the world, beginning a new age.

Conjecture: It had become traditional to have a strong separation between Jesus's suffering and the hope of resurrection. Perhaps originally the Markan community was recounting only the former before Passover and the latter only after. This shouldn't be a surprise - we still do this.

Not the only postscript

The longer ending(s) to Mark

  • Not even pretending
  • Stylistic differences
  • Timeline discontinuity: begins again at the start of the first day of the week.
  • Inconsistent details: Mary Magdalene does tell the disciples.
  • We DO have copies of Mark without this postscript.

John 21

  • John 20:30-31 feels like a summation. Almost repeated in John 21:25.
  • New information: Peter is a fisherman; Nathanael is from Cana; the sons of Zebedee.
  • New apologetics: Jesus predicted Peter wouldn't die in peace; Jesus did not predict that the beloved disciple would be alive at his return.
  • We DO NOT have copies of John without this postscript.

Matthew

  • Basically Mark, 2nd edition (new, improved ending)
  • "Fixes" some things in Mark.
  • But also introduces inconsistencies - editorial fatigue

Luke

  • Mark, Ultimate edition
  • Sometimes changes Mark significantly, including to construct alignment with his modified narrative.
  • But if he thinks Mark is wrong why doesn't he use a different source? Probably he doesn't have one.

Analogy: Continental Drift

  • The outlines of Africa and South America, combined with their alignment on a map, give the appearance of two separate landmasses which once were joined.
  • If this is not coincidence, we should be able to find other seeming continuities - geographical, biological.
  • When considering the postscript theory, we would be looking for the opposite of this.
  • More like: is there evidence that India collided into Asia?
  • We would expect to find discontinuities - it could take ages for fauna and particularly flora to diffuse across the join.

CAUTION: Evidence is always ambiguous. Proof by analogy is fraud.

  • Drama may use paradoxes to keep the audience engaged
  • Scripts may have assumed knowledge, insider knowledge, hidden clues
  • Scripts may have implied stage directions, or we may be unfamiliar with dramatic or linguistic conventions
  • Authors make mistakes (we have multi-million dollar movies with plot-holes big enough for a T-Rex to escape)
  • Copyists make mistakes
  • A revisionist will aim for continuity and consistency (including amendments to the adjacent script).

Priors

  • What is your prior that Mark's audience knew stories of resurrection appearances? 95%?
  • What about that Mark originally contained no burial and empty-tomb? 10%?

The Day of Preparation (for the Passover Sabbath)

So when Pilate heard these words, he brought Jesus out and sat down on the judgment seat at a place called The Stone Pavement, and in Aramaic Gabbatha. Now it was the  day of Preparation of the Passover.
...
Since it was the day of Preparation, and so that the bodies would not remain on the cross on the Sabbath (for that Sabbath was a high day), the Jews asked Pilate that their legs might be broken and that they might be taken away.
-- John 19:13-14,31
  • A popular explanation for John setting the crucifixion before Passover is to portray Jesus as the Passover Lamb.
  • But John changes several aspects of the burial scene, seemingly out of concern for plausibility:
    • all the crucifixion victims are taken down, at the instigation of the Jewish leaders
    • crucifixion victims would normally need some assistance to die the same day (actually Mark does infer this)
    • Joseph of Arimathea was a "secret" disciple of Jesus
    • Jesus's body is anointed during the burial
  • And there is good reason to place the crucifixion before Passover:
    it hardly seems plausible that on the sacred Passover night and day the religious men on the council would:
    hold a trial; condemn a man to death; harangue Pilate into a public execution.
  • Consequently, John doesn't specify the number of days between the burial and Mary finding the empty tomb.
  • Maybe the precise days weren't important, or even known, in the early church?
  • Although Matthew and Luke "fix" many inaccuracies in Mark, John is still finding more to fix.
  • They have no better source than the very one they find inaccurate.

Early church remembrance

  • If the (still Jewish aligned) early church had rituals for remembering Jesus's death and resurrection, on which days and times might they occur?
  • Weekly, either before or after the Sabbath (or both before and after)?
  • Annually, either before or after the Passover (or both)?
  • Annually, remembering his death at Passover and his resurrection on the first day of the week?
  • Probably a mish-mash of practices.

Speculation

  • One could imagine that initially it was remembered every week, especially in new churches or whenever there was a feeling that the Parousia was imminent.
  • Over time, it perhaps became an annual event, likely remembering the death at Passover (synchronised with the Jewish community), then the resurrection at the next church gathering (preumably the first day of the week).
  • John's timeline actually works better with this (speculated) practise.
  • But the church now adheres more to Mark's timeline.
  • Imagine if John's timeline had won - we would have Passover Eve instead of Good Friday.

Apologetics

  • What the empty-tomb and burial postscript does have is a non-stop litany of excuses.

    • Jesus had to be buried because of the impending Sabbath.
    • Jesus was really dead - it was verified by Romans.
    • Although the disciples who had followed Jesus to Jerusalem had deserted,
      there were also female followers who had followed Jesus to Jerusalem.
    • The women knew where Jesus was buried - they watched when the body was laid in the tomb.
    • The women had to return to the tomb because they needed to anoint the body.
    • When the women returned they were able to enter the tomb, but that was not because Joseph simply neglected to seal the tomb.
    • The women kept the empty tomb a secret - that's why the disciples never talked about it.
  • Are the excuses defending the resurrection?
  • No! The resurrection appearances are presumed.
  • The excuses are apologetics for the empty-tomb.
  • None of the New Testament epistles mention the empty-tomb, let alone defend it.
  • Yet from the first surviving gospel it has become a hill to die on.

Apologetic evolution

That the empty-tomb is so vigorously defended suggests it was proving effective for sustaining or growing the church.

Sustaining
Maybe some christians were becoming disillusioned with the continuously delayed Parousia and the unverifiable resurrection appearances to the long departed witnesses.
The new empty tomb rumour was compelling, but they weren't going to be fooled twice - the account had to be shored up in response to internal critique.
Growing
Perhaps the empty-tomb story was proving effective for attracting non-Christians (Jews? Gentiles?) into the fold.
This was concerning to the non-believing families and communities who sought to discredit it - the account had to evolve in response to external critique.

Timescales

  • If the apologetics predated Mark then they would almost certainly have leaked into the earlier stories in his gospel - at a bare minimum, the detail that Jesus had female disciples who followed him to Jerusalem and then to the cross.
  • This suggests late evolution of the postscript.
  • The density of the apologetics suggests rapid evolution - they come as one package.
  • Is it possible that almost all of the apologetic solutions occurred in response to problems raised in rehearsals of the play?
  • Either actual rehearsals, or the rehearsals one plays in the mind when constructing a new scenario.

Speculation regarding the evolution of the postscript
which grows consistently wilder

If the theory of the postscript is granted, how might the story have started and then evolved?

  • If the postscript contains obvious signs of rapid apologetic development, it seems reasonable to extrapolate that this apologetic development also occurred in the immediately prior non-written account.
  • The "original" gospel of Mark (and the final gospel) would typically have been consumed as dramatized readings.
  • Maybe it was originally loosely compiled from short dramas that christian communities used to perform regularly, and part of its popularity was as a resource for short plays.
  • There was a strong focus on the events leading up to the crucifixion - perhaps this reflected a church practise of remembering those events annually before Passover.
  • Surely there were also retellings and plays of resurrection encounters, but only after Passover.
  • Probably these were assumed to be in Galilee.
  • One Passover there is a novel alternative to the resurrection performance - Peter (or possibly just some anonymous members of the Eleven) goes to a tomb outside of Jerusalem and finds it empty.
  • This new scene is immediately popular, and within a few weeks has been performed in several churches.
  • As the next Passover approaches (at the original church) there is interest in integrating the resurrection prequel into the pre-existing resurrection play(s).
  • During rehearsal, it becomes apparent that the transition from the tomb to Galilee will be dramatically problematic, because Peter and the disciples are meant to be in fear and despair and completely unprepared for the appearance of Jesus.
  • Thus Peter (and / or the disciples) are replaced with women.
  • The performance is a hit and this prequel starts to be repeated outside of the retellings of resurrection encounters.
  • It is found to have evangelistic appeal, given the context of the well known christian claim that Jesus had risen and appeared to his disciples.

Speculation regarding the evolution of the postscript
which grows consistently wilder

  • At some point an ambitious church attending playwright sits down with a blank sheet of paper to create an epilogue to the gospel.
  • This, initially separate, account sparks interest, but being written down it can be subjected to stiffer critique.
  • Soon, the playwright learns to think like a critic and - with the help of a few rehearsals and written revisions - manages to develop a script for a live performance. In this revision the young man in a linen cloth links the burial scene to that of the empty tomb.
  • This still isn't quite robust but - after someone tells him that Joseph of Arimathea was known to bury crucifixion victims - he knows how to fix it. His regular audience are a little surprised that the young man is seized during Jesus's arrest rather than burial, but it isn't confusing because they've seen him perform exactly this part before.
  • This is the version of Mark which goes viral - temporarily - and becomes the template for all the narrative gospels.

The proposed revision history is derived entirely from existing texts.
However, it does propose that Mark was initially planned to end at Mark 15:39.
