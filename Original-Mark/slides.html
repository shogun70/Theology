Why did Mark go viral ...

despite not including resurrection appearances?

We find this surprising because we are looking backwards through the later gospels which included resurrection encounters. Mark's first audiences were perfectly fine not having those encounters in their Passion play.

The resurrection is an afterthought in Mark (even including the proposed postscript). Mark isn't trying to convince his audience that Jesus is the Son of God - he isn't writing that they may believe. Mark is reminding his audience how a true Messiah saves the world, beginning a new age.

Conjecture: It had become traditional to have a strong separation between Jesus's suffering and the hope of resurrection. Perhaps originally the Markan community was recounting only the former before Passover and the latter only after. (We still do this.)

Not the only postscript

The longer ending(s) to Mark

  • Not even pretending
  • Stylistic differences
  • Timeline discontinuity: begins again at the start of the first day of the week.
  • Inconsistent details: Mary Magdalene does tell the disciples.

John 21

  • John 20:30-31 feels like a summation. Almost repeated in John 21:25.
  • New information: Peter is a fisherman; Nathanael is from Cana; the sons of Zebedee.
  • New apologetics: Jesus predicted Peter wouldn't die in peace; the beloved disciple was rumored to live until Christ's return but Jesus did not say that.

Matthew

  • Basically Mark, 2nd edition (new, improved ending)
  • "Fixes" some things in Mark.
  • But also introduces inconsistencies - editorial fatigue

Luke

  • Mark, Ultimate edition
  • Sometimes changes Mark significantly, including to construct alignment with his modified narrative.
  • But if he thinks Mark is wrong why doesn't he use a different source? Probably he doesn't have one.

Because we have Mark, Matthew, and Luke, we can see the editorial process is iterative - experimentation with different approaches; change things; fix things; forget to fix things.

But what if we didn't have Mark? How would we approach Matthew?

Similarly - if we postulate our canonical Mark contains a postscript to an earlier version - what might we expect to see?

It feels like an example of Chekhov's gun somehow.

Remove everything that has no relevance to the story. If you say in the first act that there is a rifle hanging on the wall, in the second or third act it absolutely must go off. If it's not going to be fired, it shouldn't be hanging there.

Timelines

The Day of Preparation (for the Sabbath)

Jesus is apparently crucified on the Day of Preparation but this detail is not divulged until it is mentioned as the reason to get Jesus off his cross.

After three days

On three occasions Jesus predicts he will rise after three days. Yet his death is the late in the day before the Sabbath and his resurrection no later than first thing on the day after the Sabbath.
Matthew and Luke change each prediction to on the third day.
John places the death at least a day earlier, either for theological reasons or perhaps to address this discrepancy between prediction and story.

Anointing the body

When the Sabbath was past, Mary Magdalene, Mary the mother of James, and Salome bought spices, so that they might go and anoint him.
And very early on the first day of the week, when the sun had risen, they went to the tomb.
Mark 16:1-2
  • The women's reason for going to the tomb is to anoint the body, but intuitively this only makes sense before burial.
  • Matthew either forgets to include this reason or, more likely, leaves it out because it isn't satisfactory.
  • John "fixes" it by having it performed by the burial party. 
After these things Joseph of Arimathea ... came and took away his body.
Nicodemus also, who earlier had come to Jesus by night, came bringing a mixture of myrrh and aloes, about seventy-five pounds in weight.
So they took the body of Jesus and bound it in linen cloths with the spices, as is the burial custom of the Jews.
John 19:36-40
  • Anointing the body seems a spurious reason for the women to attend the tomb.
  • But that isn't evidence that the women at the empty tomb is a postscript.
And while he was at Bethany ... a woman came with an alabaster flask of ointment of pure nard, very costly, and she broke the flask and poured it over his head.

Mark 14:3-9
  • Jesus is anointed at Bethany and we are told it is for his burial. This seems to invalidate the women's reason for visiting the tomb.
  • But could this be foreshadowing?
  • It seems implausible that Mark could write that this anonymous woman would be remembered forever if he was aware of the named women at the tomb.
  • But maybe this is some form of dramatic irony - "Hey, aren't you forgetting Mary Magdalene?"

Solutions:
  • The young man in the empty-tomb scene is talking to the audience.
  • The audience knows there were other times Jesus said this.
  • There is an implied staged direction within the Last Supper scene that the women and young man are present.
  • These were later insertions intended to link the empty-tomb scene to the rest of Mark.

In the Last Supper scene, Jesus quotes scripture exemplifying his prediction that all the disciples will fall away, and Peter only responds to the same - the interaction flows better without the resurrection prediction.

Revision History

  • Mismatches and discontinuities in the script could be caused by revisions - inventing, copying, rewriting.
  • If we are postulating that the burial and empty-tomb scenes are a postscript then presumably it started simple and underwent revision while the rest of the gospel stayed stable.
  • We only have the final text, but educated guesses - based on the mismatches in the text - may allow us to step back through the revisions of the (proposed) postscript.

Location of appearances

Mark foreshadows that Jesus resurrection encounters will be for the disciples (and the women?) in Galilee.

Matthew actually shows the disciples seeing Jesus in Galilee. But the resurrection action is actually weighted towards Jerusalem: the women meet Jesus; saints are resurrected; tomb guards witness angels and Jesus meeting the women.

Luke modifies Jesus's words so that the encounters are foreshadowed for Jerusalem, and all the encounters are in or near Jerusalem.

John similarly has resurrection encounters in Jerusalem with the exception of the likely postscript of John 21.

Nature of appearances

Mark

  • Foreshadowed (not explicit)
  • Visible in a location

Matthew

  • Seen, heard, worshipped by the disciples (but some doubted)
  • Seen, heard, touched, worshipped by the women (no doubts about it)

Luke

  • A stranger on the road (??)
  • An unelaborated vision to Peter
  • A visitation to the disciples. Talks. Flesh and bones. Eats. Has crucifixion wounds. Revivification plus?
  • Taken up to heaven (ambiguous).

Acts

  • Eats, talks.
  • Taken up into the heavens. Obscured by a cloud.

John

  • Seen, touched, talked to, by Mary Magdalene.
  • A visitation to the disciples in a locked room. Talks. Has crucifixion wounds.
    Heavenly-body, earthly-body fusion? Fusion body?
  • A similar visitation to the disciples this time with the doubting Thomas.
    He is told to stop doubting and he emphatically does.
  • A stranger on the shores of the Sea of Galilee whom the disciples infer to be Jesus (??)

Resurrection outside the NT

Josephus - War of the Jews - Book 2

Historians seem to be in disagreement whether Josephus is reliable regarding religious beliefs, but the inescapable conclusion is that there is debate and a range of theories, apparently with a high correspondence to Paul's audience in 1 Corinthians 15.

Essenes

That bodies are corruptible ... but that the souls are immortal ... and that they come out of the most subtil air
but that when they are set free from the bonds of the flesh ... rejoice and mount upward.

Greeks

And indeed the Greeks seem to me to have followed the same notion,
when they allot the islands of the blessed to their brave men ...
and to the souls of the wicked, the region of the ungodly in Hades ...
which is built on this first supposition, that souls are immortal;
and thence are those exhortations to virtue, and dehortations from wickedness collected;
whereby good men are bettered in the conduct of their life by the hope they have of reward after their death,
and whereby the vehement inclinations of bad men to vice are restrained,
by the fear and expectation ... that ... they should suffer immortal punishment after their death.

Pharisees

They say that all souls are incorruptible,
but that the souls of good men only are removed into other bodies,
but that the souls of bad men are subject to eternal punishment.

Saducees

To act what is good, or what is evil, is at men’s own choice ...
They also take away the belief of the immortal duration of the soul, and the punishments and rewards in Hades.
