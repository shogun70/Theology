Timelines

The Day of Preparation (for the Sabbath)

Jesus is apparently crucified on the Day of Preparation but this detail is not divulged until it is mentioned as the reason to get Jesus off his cross.

After three days

On three occasions Jesus predicts he will rise after three days. Yet his death is the late in the day before the Sabbath and his resurrection no later than first thing on the day after the Sabbath.
Matthew and Luke change each prediction to on the third day.
John places the death at least a day earlier, either for theological reasons or perhaps to address this discrepancy between prediction and story.

Anointing the body

The women's reason for going to the tomb is to anoint the body, but you do this before burial.
Matthew addresses issue by not mentioning it. John "fixes" it by having Joseph and Nicodemus perform the anointing.

Moreover, this reason is doubly invalidated because Jesus was already anointed for burial in Bethany - a story which surely would not have been given such significance if Mark was thinking of the women at the tomb?
Or is this dramatic foreshadowing or insider knowledge?

Timestamps of Good Friday

There is a strong continuity of timestamps on the day of crucifixion:

  • Jesus is brought to Pilate "as soon as it was morning"
  • he is crucified when "it was the third hour"
  • a darkness covers the land "when the sixth hour had come"
  • he dies "at the ninth hour"
  • he is taken from the cross "when evening had come"

Once you see the pattern it is hard not to think that the timestamps were deliberately included to mark out a whole day, including the burial.
Doesn't that rule out the proposed postscript?
Maybe, but it could have been coincidence. Or the pattern could have been enhanced with some inserts into Mark 15.

Female followers

If we read the New Testament chronologically according to when it was written, this is the first occasion where it was recorded that the earthly Jesus even had female followers.

There are a few stories about anonymous women who were dignified by Jesus:

  • the women healed of bleeding
  • the Syrophoenician woman praised for faith
  • the women who anoints Jesus at Bethany

Why aren't there any stories of honor for the most faithful of Jesus's followers?

New information could point to inherent inconsistency.

Anticipated Manuscript Evidence

Less manuscripts for Mark than the later gospels.
No manuscripts definitively establishing the existence of the well accepted sayings gospel, Q.
How much less likely is it to find manuscript evidence for earlier drafts of the gospel of Mark?

No matter, let us consider what forms of manuscript evidence would be supportive of an earlier Mark without the suggested postscript, (ordered by approximate weight):

  • a fragment which ends at Mark 15:39.
  • a fragment of Mark 15 which does not contain the timestamps - the third hour, the sixth hour, the ninth hour.
  • an ending of Mark which doesn't mention the women at the cross.
  • a fragment of Mark 14 without v28 or v51.
  • an account of the Transfiguration or Jesus walking on the water which don't align with the gospels.
  • early christian writings which indicate that Mark underwent revisions. e.g. the Mar Saba letter, if found and accepted as plausibly Clementine, would support an early belief that Mark's gospel underwent revision.

Location of appearances

Mark foreshadows that Jesus resurrection encounters will be for the disciples (and the women?) in Galilee.

Matthew actually shows the disciples seeing Jesus in Galilee. But the resurrection action is actually weighted towards Jerusalem: the women meet Jesus; saints are resurrected; tomb guards witness angels and Jesus meeting the women.

Luke modifies Jesus's words so that the encounters are foreshadowed for Jerusalem, and all the encounters are in or near Jerusalem.

John similarly has resurrection encounters in Jerusalem with the exception of the likely postscript of John 21.

  • Foreshadowed (not explicit)
  • Visible in a location

  • Seen, heard, touched, worshipped by the women (no doubts about it)

  • A visitation to the disciples. Talks. Flesh and bones. Eats. Has crucifixion wounds. Revivification plus?
  • Taken up to heaven (ambiguous).

John the Baptist - Mark 6:14-29

  • The miracles of Jesus are a sign of John's resurrection. (reminiscent of transmigration)

Paul - 1 Corinthians 15

  • It is sown a natural body; it is raised a spiritual body.
  • Just as we have borne the image of the man of dust, we shall also bear the image of the man of heaven.
  • flesh and blood cannot inherit the kingdom of God

But some Corinthian christians seem to dispute Paul's version of resurrection:

  • how can some of you say that there is no resurrection of the dead?
  • “How are the dead raised? With what kind of body do they come?”

Paul thinks that will inevitably lead to immorility:

If the dead are not raised, “Let us eat and drink, for tomorrow we die.” ...
Wake up from your drunken stupor, as is right, and do not go on sinning.
